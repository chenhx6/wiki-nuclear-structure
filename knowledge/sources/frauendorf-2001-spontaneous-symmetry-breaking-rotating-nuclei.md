## Summary

The review uses the rotating mean field and cranking/Routhian formalism to organize high-spin rotational bands by the discrete symmetries left unbroken by the density, currents, and angular-momentum vector. Principal-axis, planar tilted-axis, and aplanar/chiral solutions lead to different band patterns. The same framework separates collective electric rotation from magnetic and antimagnetic rotation driven by high-j current loops, and explains band termination as progressive alignment and restoration of a rotational symmetry. The review repeatedly notes that laboratory bands require symmetry restoration and that TAC is a semiclassical mean-field description whose limits motivate particle-rotor, RPA, projection, and other beyond-mean-field treatments.

## Competing Interpretations and Limitations

- Frauendorf 2001 is a review. Historical nuclei, lifetime measurements, and cited calculations are background examples and must be traced to their original sources before being used as direct evidence.
- A mean-field orientation, order parameter, or Routhian minimum is not itself a laboratory-frame observable; angular-momentum projection and symmetry restoration are separate steps.
- TAC, PRM, cranked shell models, RPA and projection methods have different inputs and outputs. Agreement with a band pattern is model support, not a unique experimental assignment.
- Chiral doublets, magnetic rotation, antimagnetic rotation and band termination are distinct mechanisms even when all involve tilted angular-momentum geometry or weak charge deformation.
- Reflection-asymmetric correlations and parity-doublet signatures do not automatically establish a stable reflection-asymmetric or octupole-deformed shape.
