# Rotating Mean Field

## Definition

A rotating mean field is an intrinsic nuclear mean-field solution obtained in a frame rotating with angular frequency `omega`. It allows the density, currents, quasiparticle configuration and angular-momentum vector to be solved together rather than assuming a fixed laboratory rotor.

## Source-Level Boundary

Frauendorf 2001 uses the rotating mean field to connect cranking/Routhian solutions with semiclassically quantized rotational bands. The intrinsic solution is not itself a laboratory eigenstate; symmetry restoration or projection is needed for a quantum spectrum.

## Related Formalisms

- [[routhian]] and [[tilted-axis-cranking]]
- [[particle-rotor-model]] for a quantum core-plus-particle description
- [[random-phase-approximation]] for small-amplitude modes around a mean-field solution

## What It Can and Cannot Establish

It can organize deformation, quasiparticle alignment, rotational frequency and axis orientation. It does not by itself provide unique experimental configuration assignments, tunneling splittings, or transition strengths beyond the adopted semiclassical treatment.

## Necessary Assumptions

- A rotating-frame frequency and intrinsic frame are defined.
- Deformation, currents, quasiparticle configuration and angular-momentum orientation are solved consistently within the chosen approximation.

## Discriminating Observables

Routhians, moments of inertia, alignment trends, transition energies and electromagnetic strengths can be compared with the solution, subject to model assumptions.

## Supporting Evidence

- [[frauendorf-2001-spontaneous-symmetry-breaking-rotating-nuclei]]

## Counter-evidence and Competing Interpretations

Particle-rotor, shell-model, projection and RPA treatments can redistribute band mixing and transition strengths relative to a static mean field.

## Our Current Position

Keep the rotating mean field as an intrinsic theory layer and do not promote its orientations to direct laboratory observables.
