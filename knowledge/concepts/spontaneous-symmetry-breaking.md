# Spontaneous Symmetry Breaking

## Definition

The mean-field state of a rotating nucleus may have less symmetry than the two-body rotating Hamiltonian. The broken-symmetry intrinsic state is an oriented semiclassical description; laboratory states are built by quantization and, where needed, restoration of the broken symmetry.

## Rotational Consequences

The remaining discrete operations classify principal-axis, planar tilted-axis, aplanar/chiral and reflection-asymmetric solutions. Their signatures, parity patterns and possible partner bands differ. A broken symmetry is therefore a model-organizing principle, not a direct detector observable.

## Boundary

Near-degenerate bands, parity doublets or regular transition sequences do not uniquely identify the underlying broken symmetry. Tunneling, band mixing and finite-system fluctuations require methods beyond a static mean field.

## Necessary Assumptions

- The rotating mean field is treated as an intrinsic approximation to a finite quantum system.
- The remaining discrete operations and angular-momentum orientation are specified before assigning a band pattern.

## Discriminating Observables

Signature, parity, Delta I sequence, partner-band spacing and electromagnetic transition patterns can test a proposed symmetry class, but none is sufficient in isolation.

## Counter-evidence and Competing Interpretations

Band mixing, tunneling, shape coexistence, gamma vibration and configuration changes can mimic a symmetry-class fingerprint.

## Our Current Position

Use spontaneous symmetry breaking as a model-to-observable map, not as a direct experimental label.
